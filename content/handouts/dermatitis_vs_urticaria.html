Dermatitis vs. Urticaria

A Field Guide for the Newly Itchy
James R. Haden, M.D. | Allergy, Asthma and Immunology | Fort Worth, Texas

Why This Handout Exists

You have a rash. It itches. You have ended up in an allergy office, which means either someone thinks your immune system is involved, or you have run out of other specialists to blame. Either way, welcome.

The two most common rashes that walk through our door are dermatitis and urticaria. They share the same stage — your skin — and they both itch badly enough to make you question your life choices at three in the morning. But they are fundamentally different conditions, driven by different mechanisms, presenting with different morphology, and requiring different treatment strategies.

Except when they are not different. Because here is the complication your skin did not warn you about: these two conditions can overlap, coexist, mimic each other, and share enough underlying biology that the line between them is sometimes more of a suggestion than a boundary.

This handout is a field guide to telling them apart — and, equally important, to understanding why the distinction sometimes blurs and what that means for your treatment.

The Basics: What You Are Looking At

Dermatitis (Eczema)

Dermatitis — particularly atopic dermatitis, the most common form — is a disease of the skin barrier and the slow immune system. Think of your skin barrier as a brick wall. The bricks are skin cells (corneocytes). The mortar is a mixture of lipids and structural proteins, the most famous of which is filaggrin. In atopic dermatitis, the wall has problems. Sometimes the bricks are malformed. Sometimes the mortar is deficient. Sometimes both.

When the wall is compromised, two things happen simultaneously. Water leaks out, which is why eczematous skin is dry and prone to cracking. And allergens, irritants, and microbes sneak in, which triggers immune activation — specifically, a T-cell-driven inflammatory response dominated by Th2 cytokines (IL-4, IL-13, IL-31) that produces redness, swelling, and itch.

What it looks like: dry, red, scaly patches. In acute flares, the skin may weep or ooze. In chronic disease, repeated scratching thickens the skin into tough, leathery patches — a process called lichenification. The rash favors specific locations: the creases of the elbows and knees in older children and adults, the face and extensor surfaces in infants.

What it does: it stays put. A patch of eczema on your elbow today will be on your elbow tomorrow. It may get better or worse, but it does not pack up and relocate to your thigh overnight. Eczema is a homebody.

Urticaria (Hives)

Urticaria is a disease of the mast cell and the fast immune system. Mast cells are immune sentinels parked throughout your skin, loaded with granules of histamine and other mediators. When they are activated — by an allergen, a viral infection, pressure, cold, stress, or sometimes nothing identifiable at all — they degranulate, dumping histamine into the surrounding tissue. Histamine makes blood vessels leaky, and fluid floods into the upper layers of the skin, producing the raised, well-defined, often pale-centered swelling called a wheal. The surrounding redness is the flare.

What it looks like: raised, red or pink welts (wheals) that are typically round or oval, often with a pale center. They can be the size of a pencil eraser or the size of a dinner plate. The edges are usually well-defined — you can draw a line around a hive.

What it does: it moves. This is the single most useful distinguishing feature of urticaria. An individual hive appears, persists for minutes to hours (almost always less than 24 hours), and then vanishes completely — leaving normal-looking skin behind. Meanwhile, new hives appear elsewhere. The rash is a nomad. Today it is on your waist; tonight it is on your shoulders; tomorrow morning it has relocated to your thighs. This episodic wanderlust is the hallmark of urticaria.

If the swelling extends deeper — into the dermis or submucosa — you get angioedema: deeper, less well-defined swelling, often of the lips, eyelids, hands, or feet. Angioedema accompanies urticaria in roughly 40 percent of cases.

The Quick Comparison

Duration of individual lesions: Eczema lesions persist for days to weeks in the same location. Urticarial wheals resolve within 24 hours (usually much less) and appear elsewhere.

Skin surface: Eczema is dry, scaly, sometimes cracked or weeping. Urticaria is smooth-surfaced — the skin over a hive is not dry or scaly; it is swollen.

Location pattern: Eczema favors flexural creases (elbows, knees, wrists, neck). Urticaria has no fixed preference — it can appear anywhere and moves.

Response to antihistamines: Urticaria often responds dramatically to antihistamines because histamine is the primary mediator. Eczema responds poorly to antihistamines because itch in eczema is driven largely by non-histamine mediators (particularly IL-31).

Scarring: Neither typically scars, but chronic eczema can cause pigment changes and lichenification. Urticaria leaves no lasting marks — the skin returns to completely normal between episodes.

What Is Going On Under the Hood

The Traditional View

The traditional teaching separates these conditions cleanly. Eczema is a Th2-driven, barrier-defective, chronic inflammatory disease. Urticaria is a mast cell-driven, histamine-mediated, episodic vascular reaction. Different cells, different mediators, different mechanism, different treatment. Simple.

The Inconvenient Reality

Biology does not read textbooks.

Mast cells participate in both conditions. This is the single most important overlap, and it is the reason your allergist is qualified to treat both. In urticaria, the mast cell is obviously center stage — degranulation is the primary event. But mast cells are also significantly involved in atopic dermatitis. Eczematous skin contains increased numbers of mast cells, and those mast cells are releasing mediators — histamine, tryptase, cytokines, prostaglandins — that contribute to inflammation, itch, and barrier disruption. The mast cell's role in eczema has been underappreciated for decades and is now being aggressively re-examined as new therapies targeting mast cell pathways show efficacy in both conditions.

Th2 inflammation drives both. This is the molecular overlap that the pharmaceutical industry has spent billions of dollars exploiting — because it works. Both atopic dermatitis and chronic spontaneous urticaria involve excessive Th2 immune activity, particularly IL-4 and IL-13 signaling. Dupilumab, which blocks the IL-4 receptor alpha subunit (and thereby inhibits both IL-4 and IL-13 signaling), is now FDA-approved for atopic dermatitis, asthma, chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps, eosinophilic esophagitis, prurigo nodularis, COPD, and — as of 2025 — chronic spontaneous urticaria. One molecule. Seven diseases. Four medical specialties. The drug does not know what your chart says.

BTK inhibitors are being developed for both. Bruton's tyrosine kinase (BTK) is a signaling molecule inside mast cells and B cells. Inhibiting BTK reduces mast cell activation and degranulation. Drugs like remibrutinib and rilzabrutinib are in clinical trials for both chronic spontaneous urticaria and atopic dermatitis — because both diseases involve mast cell misbehavior, and BTK inhibitors do not care about diagnostic labels.

IgE is elevated in both. Total serum IgE is frequently elevated in atopic dermatitis (often dramatically so) and plays a role in mast cell sensitization in both conditions. Omalizumab (anti-IgE) is approved for chronic spontaneous urticaria and has shown benefit in severe atopic dermatitis in some studies — again, because the underlying biology overlaps.

They coexist more than chance would predict. Studies show that approximately 10 percent of patients with chronic spontaneous urticaria also have atopic dermatitis. That coexistence rate is not random. It reflects shared immunologic foundations — shared Th2 activation, shared mast cell involvement, shared IgE dysregulation, and likely shared genetic susceptibility.

The Spectrum Problem

If you have read our handout on rash classification philosophy ("Understanding Your Rash: Why the Label Matters Less Than You Think"), this section will sound familiar. If you have not, the short version is this: the immune system does not organize itself into the categories we invented for it.

Dermatitis and urticaria occupy different regions of a continuous inflammatory spectrum. Most patients are clearly in one territory or the other, and the distinction is clinically useful. But some patients live in the borderlands. They have features of both. Their rash has the distribution of eczema but the evanescence of urticaria. Or their hives leave behind dry, scaly patches that look eczematous. Or they respond partially to antihistamines and partially to topical steroids, which makes no sense if these are truly separate diseases but makes perfect sense if they share overlapping biology.

The clinical implications are practical, not philosophical. If your rash does not fit neatly into one box, we do not force it. We treat the pathways that are active in your specific skin, using the tools that target those pathways — whether those tools were originally developed for "eczema" or "urticaria."

The Diagnostic Approach

What Your Allergist Is Looking For

Morphology: What does the individual lesion look like? Is it a raised, smooth wheal (urticaria) or a flat, dry, scaly patch (dermatitis)? Are there vesicles (tiny blisters) or excoriations (scratch marks)? Is the skin thickened from chronic rubbing?

Time course: How long does an individual lesion last in one spot? This is arguably the single most useful question. If a specific lesion resolves in under 24 hours and new ones appear elsewhere, that is urticaria until proven otherwise. If it persists for days in the same location, that is eczema behavior.

Distribution: Where is it? Flexural creases suggest atopic dermatitis. Contact patterns (watchband line, earlobes from earrings, waistband) suggest contact dermatitis. Random, shifting, unpredictable locations suggest urticaria.

Triggers: What makes it worse? Specific allergen exposures, irritants, and barrier disruption point toward dermatitis. Physical stimuli (pressure, cold, heat, vibration), infections, stress, and "nothing I can identify" point toward urticaria.

Response to treatment: This is diagnostic data, not just therapeutic outcome. If antihistamines completely control the rash, mast cell degranulation was the dominant pathway — urticaria. If antihistamines barely touch it but topical steroids or calcineurin inhibitors help, T-cell-driven inflammation is dominant — dermatitis. If both help partially, both pathways are active. Congratulations: you live in the borderlands.

The Tests

For dermatitis: Patch testing identifies contact allergens (metals, fragrances, preservatives). Total IgE and specific IgE panels assess atopic status. Skin biopsy, if needed, shows spongiosis (intercellular edema in the epidermis) with a lymphocytic infiltrate.

For urticaria: Complete blood count with differential, ESR or CRP, thyroid antibodies, and sometimes C4 or tryptase levels. Autologous serum skin test or basophil activation testing can identify autoimmune urticaria. Biopsy of urticaria shows dermal edema with perivascular mixed infiltrate — a pattern distinct from eczema.

For the borderlands: Sometimes both workups, and a willingness to treat empirically and adjust based on response.

The Treatment Toolbox

For Dermatitis

Barrier repair is foundational. Moisturize aggressively — twice daily minimum, immediately after bathing to lock in hydration. Fragrance-free, dye-free everything: soap, detergent, moisturizer. Lukewarm showers (hot water strips the already compromised barrier). Cotton clothing against the skin. Gloves for wet work.

Topical anti-inflammatories for flares. Topical corticosteroids remain the first-line treatment for eczema flares — matched to severity and location (mild for the face, stronger for the body, nothing too potent in skin folds). Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) are steroid-sparing alternatives especially useful for delicate areas. Crisaborole (a PDE4 inhibitor) is another non-steroidal option.

Systemic therapy for moderate-to-severe disease. Dupilumab has transformed the treatment of moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis. JAK inhibitors (upadacitinib, abrocitinib, baricitinib) offer oral alternatives. Tralokinumab (anti-IL-13) is another biologic option. For refractory cases, cyclosporine, methotrexate, or mycophenolate may be used.

Antihistamines have a limited role. They may help with nighttime itch enough to improve sleep, but they do not address the underlying inflammation. This is a key difference from urticaria management.

For Urticaria

Antihistamines are the foundation. Second-generation H1 antihistamines (cetirizine, levocetirizine, fexofenadine, loratadine, desloratadine, bilastine) at standard doses first, then uptitrated to up to four times the standard dose if needed. This is guideline-recommended, safe, and effective for many patients. H2 blockers (famotidine) can be added for additional histamine blockade.

Omalizumab for antihistamine-refractory disease. Anti-IgE therapy reduces mast cell activation and is FDA-approved for chronic spontaneous urticaria that does not respond to antihistamines.

Dupilumab — the newest addition. FDA-approved for chronic spontaneous urticaria in 2025, dupilumab blocks the IL-4/IL-13 pathway that drives both urticaria and eczema. Its approval for CSU is therapeutic proof that these conditions share biology.

Short courses of systemic corticosteroids (3–5 days) can rescue severe flares but are not appropriate for long-term management.

Cyclosporine is an option for severe, refractory chronic urticaria when other therapies fail.

Trigger identification and avoidance where possible — though in chronic spontaneous urticaria, no trigger is identifiable in the majority of cases, which is why it is called "spontaneous."

For the Borderlands

When features of both conditions coexist, treatment addresses both pathways. Barrier repair plus antihistamines. Topical anti-inflammatories for the eczematous component plus systemic antihistamines for the urticarial component. And increasingly, therapies like dupilumab that work across both conditions — because they target shared biology rather than a diagnostic label.

Self-Care That Actually Works

Keep a rash journal. Document what your rash looks like, where it appears, how long individual lesions last, and what you were doing, eating, wearing, or stressed about when it appeared. Photograph it — your rash will vanish the moment you arrive at the doctor's office, because rashes have a perverse sense of timing. A photo taken at 2 AM is worth more than a description at 10 AM.

Clip your nails short. Nighttime scratching is unconscious, vigorous, and destructive. Short nails cause less damage. Cotton gloves at night can help for eczema.

Cool compresses tame hives. Histamine-mediated itch responds to cold. A cool washcloth or wrapped ice pack applied to active hives can provide rapid relief. (Note: if you have cold urticaria — hives triggered by cold — this is obviously counterproductive. Know your triggers.)

Fragrance-free everything. Detergent, soap, shampoo, moisturizer, dryer sheets. Fragrance is the most common contact allergen in the developed world and a frequent trigger for both eczema flares and urticarial episodes.

Stress management counts. This is not a dismissal — it is immunology. Psychological stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and the sympathetic nervous system, both of which modulate mast cell behavior and T-cell function. Stress does not cause your rash, but it measurably worsens it. Whatever helps you decompress — exercise, meditation, therapy, walking the dog — is a legitimate component of rash management.

When to Call Us

⚠ Urgently (same day or emergency) Any lip, tongue, or throat swelling. Difficulty breathing. Lightheadedness or feeling faint with hives. These are angioedema or anaphylaxis, not garden-variety hives, and they require immediate medical attention. Use your epinephrine auto-injector if prescribed and call 911.

Soon (within a week): Rash spreading rapidly. Blistering. Signs of skin infection (pus, honey-colored crusting, increasing pain, warmth, or red streaking). Hives persisting beyond six weeks despite over-the-counter antihistamines.

At your next visit: Needing oral steroids more than twice in six months. Over-the-counter medications no longer controlling symptoms. New patterns emerging. Questions about whether your treatment plan needs adjustment.

The Bottom Line Dermatitis is mostly a barrier-plus-slow-immune-fire problem. Seal the wall, soothe the flames. Urticaria is mostly a fast, histamine-fueled sprinkler show. Block the nozzles, track the triggers. But "mostly" is doing heavy lifting in both of those sentences. These conditions share mast cells, share Th2 cytokines, share IgE dysregulation, and increasingly share treatments. The clean separation between them is clinically useful — it tells us where to start. But it is not biologically absolute, and when your rash does not respect the boundary, neither should your treatment plan. Both are treatable. Both can be chronic. Both benefit from teamwork — you, your allergist, maybe a dermatologist, and the understanding that your skin is telling a story. Our job is to read it accurately, even when the punctuation is ambiguous.
