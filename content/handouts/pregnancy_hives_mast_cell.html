Pregnancy, Hives, and Mast Cell Disease

What You Need to Know Before, During, and After

Pregnancy changes everything. Your shoes don’t fit. Your sense of smell could detect a gas leak three counties away. You cry at commercials for paper towels. And if you happen to be a woman with chronic hives, mast cell activation syndrome, or a mast cell disorder, your body has decided to run what amounts to an immunological science experiment — on you — for the next nine months.

The good news is that most women with urticaria and mast cell disease have safe pregnancies and healthy babies. The less convenient news is that pregnancy can change the rules of a game you’d only just figured out how to play. Medications that were working may need to be reconsidered. Symptoms that were stable may shift. And your immune system, which was already unreliable, is about to be remodeled from the ground up to accommodate a tiny human who is technically half foreign tissue.

This handout is written specifically for women with hives, mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), or systemic mastocytosis who are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or who just emerged from pregnancy wondering what on earth happened. It covers the immunology, the medications, the delivery planning, and the practical details that no one tells you until you’re already in the thick of it.

What Pregnancy Does to Your Immune System

To understand why hives and mast cell disease behave unpredictably during pregnancy, you need to understand one fundamental biological fact: your baby is immunologically foreign. Half of that child’s genetic material came from someone else, and under normal circumstances your immune system would reject foreign tissue with great enthusiasm. The fact that it doesn’t — that the pregnancy survives at all — is one of the more remarkable feats of human biology.

Pregnancy accomplishes this through a deliberate and dramatic shift in immune function. The system tilts away from Th1 responses (the aggressive, cell-killing arm that fights infections and rejects transplants) and toward Th2 responses (the arm that handles parasites, produces antibodies, and — inconveniently for our purposes — drives allergic inflammation). Regulatory T cells flood the uterine lining to maintain tolerance of the fetus. The whole immune apparatus is restructured.

For women with allergic disease, this Th2 shift is a double-edged sword. Some allergic conditions improve during pregnancy because the overall immune tone is suppressive. Others worsen because the Th2 pathway — the very pathway that drives hives, eczema, and allergic inflammation — is being amplified. The classic teaching about asthma applies here too: roughly one-third of women improve during pregnancy, one-third worsen, and one-third stay about the same.

The hormonal changes compound the picture. Estrogen rises dramatically and steadily throughout pregnancy, reaching levels many times higher than anything the menstrual cycle ever produced. Progesterone does the same. Both hormones interact directly with mast cells. Estrogen, acting through estrogen receptor-alpha, can enhance histamine release from mast cells and basophils. Progesterone, somewhat paradoxically, tends to suppress mast cell degranulation in laboratory studies — but in a living, breathing pregnant woman, the picture is more complex than any test tube would suggest.

The net result is unpredictability. Some women with chronic hives find that pregnancy quiets their symptoms. Others find that pregnancy makes them worse. And some women who never had hives before discover them for the first time during pregnancy. If this seems unsatisfying from a scientific standpoint, that’s because it is. The honest answer is that we cannot reliably predict which direction any individual woman’s mast cells will go.

Chronic Urticaria in Pregnancy

Chronic spontaneous urticaria — hives lasting more than six weeks without an identifiable external trigger — affects women roughly twice as often as men. This sex disparity is itself a clue that hormones play a role, and it means that a significant number of CSU patients are women of childbearing age who will eventually face the question of how pregnancy affects their disease.

The data we have suggest that chronic urticaria does not follow a single predictable pattern during pregnancy. Some women experience remission. Others experience flares, particularly in the first trimester when hormonal changes are most rapid. Still others see no change at all. There is no reliable predictor of which category a given patient will fall into, though women whose hives tend to fluctuate with their menstrual cycle may be more likely to see changes during pregnancy.

What we can say with confidence is that chronic urticaria is not dangerous to the fetus. Hives are uncomfortable, sleep-disrupting, and demoralizing, but they do not cross the placenta, do not cause birth defects, and do not threaten the pregnancy itself. The main risk comes not from the hives but from undertreatment — a woman so miserable she can’t eat, sleep, or function is not having a healthy pregnancy either.

The Autoimmune Subtype

About forty percent of patients with chronic spontaneous urticaria have an autoimmune component — IgG antibodies directed against the high-affinity IgE receptor on mast cells, or against IgE itself. Women are disproportionately represented in this subgroup. Given that pregnancy alters autoimmune activity in complex ways (some autoimmune diseases improve during pregnancy, others worsen), the autoimmune urticaria subtype adds another layer of unpredictability. There is no published evidence that autoimmune CSU is more dangerous during pregnancy than the non-autoimmune form. It may, however, be more resistant to standard antihistamine therapy, which can complicate management.

Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) and Pregnancy

MCAS is the condition that keeps allergists and immunologists up at night even when their patients aren’t pregnant. It is a disorder of excessive mast cell mediator release — histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, and a cascade of other chemicals — in response to triggers that may be identifiable or entirely cryptic. Symptoms span multiple organ systems: flushing, hives, gastrointestinal distress, tachycardia, blood pressure swings, brain fog, bone pain, and in severe cases, anaphylaxis.

Now add pregnancy.

How Pregnancy Affects MCAS

The honest scientific answer is that we don’t fully know. MCAS as a formally recognized diagnosis is relatively young, and large-scale studies of MCAS in pregnancy do not exist. What we have are case series, expert opinion, and extrapolation from related conditions like systemic mastocytosis.

Suspected pattern (incomplete understanding): Pregnancy appears to have a variable effect on MCAS, much like its effect on chronic urticaria. Some women report improvement — possibly because the high progesterone state has a stabilizing effect on mast cells, or because the generalized immune suppression of pregnancy dampens the overactive pathways. Other women report worsening, particularly in the first trimester and again in the third trimester, when hormonal fluctuations are greatest and physical stressors (nausea, fatigue, compression of abdominal organs) may themselves act as mast cell triggers. A subset of women experience their first recognizable MCAS symptoms during pregnancy, which can make diagnosis especially challenging since many MCAS symptoms overlap with normal pregnancy complaints.

The Diagnostic Challenge

Here is the uncomfortable truth about MCAS in pregnancy: nausea, flushing, tachycardia, fatigue, abdominal bloating, and lightheadedness are all symptoms of MCAS. They are also symptoms of being pregnant. Separating the two requires clinical judgment, a thorough history, and sometimes a tryptase level drawn during a symptomatic episode (though tryptase can be normal in MCAS, which helps approximately no one).

The key distinguishing features are pattern and severity. Normal pregnancy nausea is worst in the first trimester and tends to improve. MCAS-driven nausea may persist, fluctuate unpredictably, or respond to antihistamines in a way that morning sickness typically does not. Flushing that occurs in discrete episodes, particularly with identifiable triggers, is more suggestive of mast cell activation than the generalized warmth of pregnancy. Hives during pregnancy are not normal and should always prompt consideration of mast cell disease, particularly if they recur.

The Anaphylaxis Question

The concern that looms largest for MCAS patients contemplating pregnancy is anaphylaxis. Mast cell disease carries a baseline risk of severe allergic reactions, and pregnancy introduces new variables: changes in blood volume, changes in drug metabolism, the presence of a fetus who will be affected by maternal hemodynamic collapse, and the stress of labor and delivery itself.

The reassuring data comes from systemic mastocytosis, a related but distinct condition. Published case series of mastocytosis in pregnancy report that severe anaphylaxis during pregnancy is rare, though not zero. Most women with well-managed mastocytosis have uneventful deliveries. The key is planning: your allergist, obstetrician, and anesthesiologist should all know your diagnosis before you arrive in labor and delivery.

For MCAS specifically, the data is thinner, but the principles are the same. Women with a history of anaphylaxis should carry epinephrine at all times during pregnancy. Epinephrine is safe in pregnancy — the risk of untreated anaphylaxis to both mother and fetus far exceeds any risk from the medication. This is not a close call. If you are having anaphylaxis, use your epinephrine.

Pregnancy-Specific Rashes That Mimic Hives

Pregnancy has its own dermatological vocabulary, and some conditions that occur exclusively during pregnancy can look enough like hives to cause confusion.

PUPPP (Pruritic Urticarial Papules and Plaques of Pregnancy)

PUPPP is the most common pregnancy-specific skin eruption, occurring in roughly one in 160 pregnancies. It typically appears in the third trimester, starting within the stretch marks of the abdomen and spreading outward to the thighs and arms. Despite the word “urticarial” in the name, PUPPP is not true urticaria — it does not involve mast cell degranulation, it does not respond to the triggers that provoke chronic hives, and it resolves spontaneously after delivery. It is more common in first pregnancies and in women carrying twins or triplets.

PUPPP is intensely itchy but poses no risk to the baby. Treatment is symptomatic: topical corticosteroid creams, cool compresses, and the grim patience of knowing it will end when the pregnancy does.

For women who already have chronic urticaria or MCAS, the appearance of PUPPP on top of their existing condition can be maddening — and diagnostically confusing. The distinguishing feature is location and timing: PUPPP starts in striae, respects the periumbilical area (it typically doesn’t appear right around the belly button), and is confined to pregnancy. True urticaria migrates, comes and goes, and has a history predating the pregnancy.

Pemphigoid Gestationis

Much rarer (one in 50,000 pregnancies) but more important to recognize, pemphigoid gestationis begins as an itchy rash around the navel in the second or third trimester, then evolves into blisters. Unlike PUPPP, it involves an autoimmune mechanism — antibodies against the basement membrane zone of the skin. It can recur in subsequent pregnancies and may be associated with slightly increased risk of prematurity and low birth weight. It requires medical management, usually with topical or occasionally systemic corticosteroids.

Hereditary Angioedema and Pregnancy

While not hives, hereditary angioedema (HAE) deserves mention because swelling episodes may worsen during pregnancy, and the condition requires specific planning for labor and delivery.

HAE types I and II are caused by deficiency or dysfunction of C1 esterase inhibitor. Estrogen increases the activity of the contact pathway that generates bradykinin — the molecule responsible for HAE swelling — which means rising estrogen levels during pregnancy can increase attack frequency. Published data suggest that roughly one-third of women with HAE experience more frequent attacks during pregnancy, one-third experience fewer, and one-third see no change. Attacks may be more common in the first trimester, when estrogen is rising most rapidly, and in the abdomen, where the expanding uterus provides ample opportunity for microtrauma.

The critical point for delivery planning: C1 esterase inhibitor concentrate should be available in the delivery suite. Delivery itself rarely provokes attacks, but traumatic deliveries (forceps, vacuum extraction) and intubation carry higher risk. HAE medications like danazol are absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy due to virilizing effects on the fetus, but C1 inhibitor concentrate has been used safely.

Type III HAE (factor XII mutation, estrogen-dependent) is overwhelmingly a disease of women and is strongly influenced by estrogen. Pregnancy can trigger first attacks or worsen existing disease. Management is complex and should involve a specialist experienced in HAE.

Medications: What’s Safe, What’s Not, and What We Don’t Know

Medication management during pregnancy is where the rubber meets the road. Every medication decision involves balancing the risk of the drug against the risk of uncontrolled disease. For mast cell conditions, uncontrolled disease means not just discomfort but potential anaphylaxis, severe sleep deprivation, inability to eat, and psychological distress — none of which are good for a developing pregnancy.

Antihistamines

H1 antihistamines are the backbone of treatment for hives and MCAS, and the safety data in pregnancy is reassuring.

Cetirizine (Zyrtec) and loratadine (Claritin) are the most widely recommended second-generation antihistamines in pregnancy. Both have extensive human data with no increased risk of birth defects. Loratadine has traditionally been favored in some guidelines because of its long track record, but cetirizine has equivalent safety data and may be more effective for hives.

Among the older first-generation antihistamines, chlorpheniramine has the longest safety record in pregnancy and is generally considered the preferred choice when a sedating antihistamine is needed. Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) has also been used for decades with reassuring data, though its sedating effects are a limitation for daytime use.

H2 antihistamines like famotidine are sometimes used as adjunctive therapy in mast cell disease. Famotidine has limited but reassuring pregnancy data. It may be continued if it’s a critical part of a patient’s MCAS regimen, but this decision should be made in consultation with your allergist and obstetrician.

Cromolyn Sodium

Oral cromolyn sodium, a mast cell stabilizer, is sometimes used in MCAS for gastrointestinal symptoms. It is minimally absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract, which makes it among the safest options in pregnancy. Inhaled cromolyn for asthma also has a long and reassuring safety record.

Corticosteroids

Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone, prednisolone) may be necessary for severe urticaria or mast cell flares that don’t respond to antihistamines. They are not risk-free in pregnancy — prolonged use has been associated with slightly increased risk of cleft palate (when used in the first trimester), gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, and low birth weight. However, in severe disease, the benefits of treatment generally outweigh these risks. Short courses are preferred over chronic use whenever possible.

Topical corticosteroid creams for itchy rashes are generally safe in pregnancy, particularly in mild to moderate potencies.

Epinephrine

Epinephrine is safe and should be used without hesitation for anaphylaxis during pregnancy. The risk of maternal and fetal hypoxia from untreated anaphylaxis vastly exceeds any risk from the medication. Women with MCAS or mastocytosis should carry an epinephrine auto-injector throughout pregnancy and have a clear action plan.

Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists

Montelukast (Singulair) has limited human pregnancy data, but animal studies are reassuring and it is sometimes continued in patients whose mast cell disease is well-controlled on a regimen that includes it. This is a case-by-case decision.

Omalizumab (Xolair)

Omalizumab, an anti-IgE monoclonal antibody used for chronic urticaria and severe allergic asthma, has accumulating pregnancy data from registries that has been largely reassuring — no increased rate of major birth defects or adverse pregnancy outcomes compared to the general population. However, it is not formally approved for use in pregnancy, and the decision to continue it must weigh the severity of the disease against the limitations of the available data. For women with severe chronic urticaria or MCAS that is well-controlled only on omalizumab, discontinuation may lead to significant disease flare.

Medications to Avoid

Danazol (used in hereditary angioedema) is absolutely contraindicated — it causes virilization of a female fetus. Methotrexate, occasionally used in severe autoimmune urticaria, is a known teratogen and must be stopped well before conception. Hydroxychloroquine, sometimes used off-label for urticarial vasculitis, has a more favorable safety profile and may be continued in select cases under specialist guidance.

Planning for Labor and Delivery

For women with mast cell disease, the delivery room is not the place for improvisation. A written plan, developed in advance with your allergist, obstetrician, and anesthesiologist, can prevent the kind of last-minute scrambling that nobody wants during childbirth.

Key Elements of a Delivery Plan

Your medical team should know your diagnosis, your baseline symptoms, your current medications, your known triggers, and your history of anaphylaxis (if any). Epinephrine should be immediately available — not in the pharmacy, not in the supply room, but in the delivery suite.

Premedication protocols may be appropriate for women with a history of severe mast cell reactions. This might include an H1 antihistamine, an H2 antihistamine, and in some cases a corticosteroid given before procedures such as cesarean section, induction of labor, or epidural placement.

Anesthetic choices should be discussed in advance. Epidural anesthesia is generally well-tolerated in mast cell patients and has the advantage of reducing the stress response that can trigger mast cell degranulation. General anesthesia introduces more potential mast cell triggers (certain neuromuscular blocking agents, opioids, rapid temperature changes) and should be avoided if possible, though it can be managed safely with appropriate precautions.

Latex allergy, which co-occurs with mast cell disease more frequently than in the general population, should be assessed, and a latex-free environment ensured if relevant.

Postpartum Considerations

The hormonal crash that follows delivery — progesterone and estrogen plummeting within hours — can itself trigger a mast cell flare. Women should be monitored postpartum and should continue their mast cell medications without interruption. Breastfeeding is generally compatible with cetirizine, loratadine, and famotidine, all of which pass into breast milk in very small quantities. Cromolyn sodium, being minimally absorbed, is also considered compatible.

Breastfeeding and Mast Cell Medications

Most women with mast cell disease can breastfeed safely while continuing their core medications. The key principles are that medications which are poorly absorbed (cromolyn), have low milk transfer (cetirizine, loratadine), or have extensive safe use histories (chlorpheniramine) are generally appropriate. Newer or less-studied medications should be discussed individually with your allergist and pediatrician.

Inhaled medications for asthma — beta-agonists, inhaled corticosteroids — transfer minimally into breast milk. Oral corticosteroids at low doses are considered compatible with breastfeeding, though timing doses after nursing can further minimize infant exposure.

The Bottom Line

Pregnancy with hives or mast cell disease is manageable. It is not risk-free — but then, no pregnancy is. The critical elements are planning, communication between specialists, and a willingness to treat symptoms aggressively enough that the mother remains functional, nourished, and rested. The immune system’s behavior during pregnancy is fascinating, unpredictable, and occasionally exasperating. Your mast cells may calm down. They may act up. They may do both in the same trimester. But with the right team, the right medications, and a delivery plan that accounts for your diagnosis, the overwhelming majority of women with mast cell disease have safe pregnancies and healthy children. The baby will keep you up at night soon enough. Your mast cells don’t need to start early.

EMERGENCY

If you experience anaphylaxis during pregnancy — hives with difficulty breathing, throat tightness, dizziness, or loss of consciousness — use your epinephrine auto-injector immediately and call 911. Epinephrine is safe in pregnancy. Untreated anaphylaxis is not. Do not hesitate.
